# The Polyglot Method: Dmitry Petrov's Ultimate Framework for Rapid Language Acquisition

---

## Who Is Dmitry Yuryevich Petrov?

**Dmitry Yuryevich Petrov** (born July 16, 1958, Novomoskovsk, Russia) is a Russian polyglot, psycholinguist, simultaneous interpreter, lecturer, and language teacher. He speaks over 30 languages, does professional simultaneous interpretation in 8 of them, and can read in approximately 50 languages. He graduated from and lectures at Moscow State Linguistic University. He served as interpreter for Soviet and Russian presidents including Gorbachev, Yeltsin, and Putin. He is the author of the book *"The Magic of the Word"* and the creator of the **"Polyglot 16"** method.

In 2012, he launched the reality show **"Polyglot"** on Russia's Kultura (Culture) TV channel, where he taught groups of 8 beginners to speak a foreign language in 16 one-hour lessons. The show covered English, German, French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Hindi, and Chinese. In 2014, Petrov won the prestigious **TEFI television award** for this show. He also founded the **Dmitry Petrov's Innovative Communication Linguistics Center**.

## The Core Philosophy

Petrov's approach is built on a few foundational beliefs that challenge traditional language education:

### 1. Anyone Can Learn — The Barrier Is Psychological, Not Intellectual

> If you can speak your native language more or less decently, you are by definition capable of speaking another one — at least at a basic level. Only a lack of motivation can serve as a limiter.

The single biggest obstacle is **fear** — fear of making mistakes, fear of sounding foolish, fear of the unknown. Before any grammar or vocabulary is introduced, this emotional wall must be broken.

### 2. Freedom Before Correctness

Petrov's motto: **"First learn to speak a foreign language, then learn to speak it correctly."**

This means from minute one, learners produce sentences. They don't start with phonetics drills, alphabet memorization, or grammar theory. They speak — badly, slowly, with pauses — but they speak. Correctness is polished later; the ability to communicate is built first.

### 3. Language Has a "Multiplication Table"

Every language has a small set of **basic algorithms** — a matrix, a skeleton structure — that governs how sentences are built. Petrov calls this the **"multiplication table" of the language.** Once you internalize this table to the level of automatism (like you know 7×8=56 without thinking), you can generate an enormous number of sentences.

### 4. 90% of Speech = 300–350 Words

Regardless of a person's age, education, or language, approximately **300–350 words** make up **90% of everything they say**. The Polyglot course focuses exclusively on these high-frequency words — not thousands of rare terms.

### 5. Regularity > Volume

**"The regularity of repetition is more important than the amount of time you spend studying."**

5 minutes of practice several times per day beats a 3-hour marathon session once a week. Consistency wires patterns into long-term memory.

## The Central Matrix: The Verb Table

This is the **heart and engine** of Petrov's entire method. It is introduced in **Lesson 1** and everything else builds on top of it.

### The Structure: 3 × 3 Grid

Every sentence in any language operates within a simple grid of:

- **3 Tenses**: Past, Present, Future
- **3 Forms**: Affirmative (+), Negative (−), Question (?)

This gives you **9 sentence types** — and that's the whole foundation.

### English Verb Table Example (using "love")

| | **Past** | **Present** | **Future** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **+ (Affirmative)** | I love**d** | I love | I **will** love |
| **− (Negative)** | I **did not** love | I **do not** love | I **will not** love |
| **? (Question)** | **Did** I love? | **Do** I love? | **Will** I love? |

**Key observations Petrov teaches:**

- In the **affirmative past**, the verb changes (add -ed or irregular form)
- In **negative and question forms**, a helper word appears (did/do/will) and the verb returns to its base form
- For **he/she/it** in present tense, add **-s** to the verb and use **does/doesn't** for negative and questions
- The pattern is **universal** — it works for ANY verb

### The "To Be" Table (Lesson 3)

Petrov creates a separate but parallel table for the verb "to be," which behaves differently:

| | **Past** | **Present** | **Future** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | was | am | will be |
| **he/she/it** | was | is | will be |
| **you/we/they** | were | are | will be |

Negative: add **not** after the verb (was not, am not, will not be)  
Question: invert the subject and verb (Was I? Am I? Will I be?)

## The Drill Method: How to Use the Table

Petrov's specific instructions for internalizing the matrix:

1. **Take one verb** (e.g., "love")
2. **Run it through all 9 cells** of the table (affirmative/negative/question × past/present/future) — this takes **20–30 seconds**
3. **Take the next verb** and repeat
4. Do this with the **core verb list** (see below)
5. Practice **multiple times per day** in short bursts (5 minutes each)
6. After 2–4 days of drilling, the structure begins to work **automatically**

The goal is not to "understand" the table — that happens in minutes. The goal is to **automate** it so deeply that constructing a sentence feels as effortless as breathing.

## The Core 30 "Magic" Verbs

Petrov argues that everyday speech uses no more than **50–60 verbs**, and he starts students with approximately **30 essential verbs**. These are the verbs introduced across the first lessons:

| Verb | Verb | Verb |
|---|---|---|
| love | live | work |
| open | close | see (saw) |
| come (came) | go (went) | know (knew) |
| think (thought) | start | finish |
| want | take | give |
| speak | tell | ask |
| make | do | have |
| get | put | read |
| write | eat | drink |
| sleep | buy | sell |

Students drill each of these through the 9-cell verb table until automatism is reached. Irregular past forms (saw, came, went, knew, thought) are memorized individually.

## The 16-Lesson Roadmap

Each lesson is approximately 45 minutes and builds directly on the previous one:

### Phase 1: The Foundation (Lessons 1–4)

**Lesson 1 — The Basic Verb Scheme**
- Psychological warm-up: identify what has blocked you from learning
- Introduction of the 3×3 verb matrix (past/present/future × affirmative/negative/question)
- First set of core verbs
- Students begin constructing simple sentences immediately
- **Homework**: Drill the verb table until automatic (5 min, several times daily)

**Lesson 2 — Expanding Sentences**
- Same verb scheme, now combined with **pronouns in 2 forms** (subject: I/he/they + object: me/him/them)
- **Interrogative words**: who, what, where, when, why, how
- **Prepositions of direction**: to, from, in, at
- Building more complex sentences

**Lesson 3 — The Verb "To Be"**
- Separate table for "to be" (am/is/are, was/were, will be)
- How "to be" differs from regular verbs
- Descriptions, states, professions ("I am a student," "She was tired")

**Lesson 4 — Articles, Prepositions & Talking About Yourself**
- Correct use of **articles** (a/an, the)
- Common **prepositions** (in, on, at, with, without, about)
- Personal topics: students describe themselves, their lives, their interests
- Review and reinforcement of all previous structures

### Phase 2: Building Blocks (Lessons 5–8)

**Lesson 5 — Possessives and Adjectives**
- My/your/his/her/our/their
- Basic adjectives for description
- Expanding vocabulary around personal topics

**Lesson 6 — Numbers, Time, Days, Months**
- Counting, telling time
- Days of the week, months, seasons
- Talking about schedules and routines

**Lesson 7 — Comparative Structures**
- More/less, bigger/smaller, better/worse
- Expressing preferences and opinions

**Lesson 8 — Modal Verbs and Extended Grammar**
- Can/could, must/should, may/might
- Expanding the range of what students can express
- Polite requests, ability, obligation

### Phase 3: Free Communication (Lessons 9–12)

**Lessons 9–10 — Situational Conversations**
- Travel, restaurants, shopping, directions
- Students engage in creative free communication on personal topics
- Teacher does NOT rush to correct mistakes — the priority is comfort and fluency

**Lesson 11 — Revision and Vocabulary Building**
- Full review of all grammatical structures from lessons 1–10
- Introduction of the **30 "magic" verbs** as a complete set
- Mechanisms for building vocabulary efficiently

**Lesson 12 — The "-tion/-sion" Trick**
- In Russian (and many other languages), roughly 50,000 words end in "-ция" / "-сия"
- In English, the same roots appear with the ending **-tion** or **-sion**
- Result: you already "know" tens of thousands of English words (information, situation, revolution, communication...)

### Phase 4: Consolidation and Confidence (Lessons 13–16)

**Lessons 13–14 — Complex Sentence Building**
- Combining multiple clauses
- Conditional structures (if... then...)
- Past continuous, present perfect (light introduction)

**Lessons 15–16 — Free Expression and Integration**
- Extended conversations on any topic
- Students demonstrate ability to express complex thoughts
- Review of all core structures
- Building a personalized study plan for continued learning

## The 7 Core Principles (Summary)

| # | Principle | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Remove the Fear** | The psychological barrier is the #1 obstacle. Break it before teaching anything. |
| 2 | **Freedom Before Correctness** | Speak first, fix later. Communication > perfection. |
| 3 | **The Matrix / Multiplication Table** | Every language has a core structural skeleton (verb table). Learn it and automate it. |
| 4 | **Automatism Through Drilling** | Understanding is not enough. You must drill until structures fire without conscious thought. |
| 5 | **High-Frequency Focus** | 300–350 words = 90% of speech. 50–60 verbs = all daily actions. Focus only on these. |
| 6 | **Contextual & Practical Learning** | No isolated grammar exercises. Everything is practiced in real conversational situations. |
| 7 | **Regularity Over Volume** | 5 minutes × 5 times/day > 1 hour × 1 time/week. Short, frequent repetition wins. |

## The Universal Applicability

A key strength of Petrov's method is that **the same framework applies to any language**. The TV show demonstrated this across English, German, French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Hindi, and Chinese. The specific verb table changes per language, but the approach is identical:

1. Identify the language's verb conjugation matrix
2. Map it into the 3×3 grid (tense × form)
3. Select the 30–60 highest-frequency verbs
4. Drill them through the grid to automatism
5. Add 300 core vocabulary words in context
6. Practice in real conversational situations

For each new language, Petrov creates a **language-specific verb table** that serves as that language's "multiplication table."

## Petrov's Study Recommendations

Based on the show and supplementary materials, Petrov recommends:

1. **Allocate more than 1 hour per lesson** — pause frequently to take notes and repeat
2. **Keep a dedicated language notebook** — write down all tables, verbs, and new words by hand
3. **Review notes daily** — even on rest days, spend 5–10 minutes reviewing what you've written
4. **Don't watch lessons back-to-back** — absorb one lesson, drill for a day, then move on
5. **Mark unclear items** in a contrasting color and return to them before the next lesson
6. **Spend 20–30 minutes twice a week** specifically on learning new vocabulary
7. **Practice speaking** — talk to friends, watch movies, read adapted texts
8. **Don't force memorization** — if you struggle with a word, skip it and return later

## Strengths of the Method

- **Extremely fast entry** — students produce sentences within the first hour
- **Psychologically supportive** — removes intimidation and builds confidence
- **Structurally elegant** — the verb table is genuinely simple and universal
- **Accessible to anyone** — no prerequisites, works for all ages
- **Proven across languages** — successfully applied to 8+ languages on the show
- **Creates a "fireproof" foundation** — even if you stop studying, the core never fully disappears

---

## Honest Limitations

- **Not a complete course** — 16 hours provides a strong foundation, not fluency. Petrov himself says additional regular study is required.
- **Vocabulary is minimal** — 300 words is enough for basic communication, but real proficiency requires 3,000–5,000+ words
- **Reading and writing skills** are not deeply addressed — the focus is on speaking and comprehension
- **Advanced grammar** (conditionals, passive voice, subjunctive) is only lightly touched
- **Requires self-discipline** — the method works only if you actually drill. Simply watching the lessons passively yields little
- **Not a "magic pill"** — reaching true intermediate level still requires 1–2 years of consistent practice

## How to Apply This Framework Right Now

### Step 1: Choose Your Target Language
Pick one language. Do not try to learn multiple simultaneously as a beginner.

### Step 2: Build Your Verb Table
Find or create the 3×3 matrix (tense × form) for your target language. This is your "multiplication table."

### Step 3: Get Your First 30 Verbs
Find the 30 most frequently used verbs in that language. These are your raw materials.

### Step 4: Drill Daily
Take each verb and run it through all 9 cells. 20–30 seconds per verb. Do this several times a day in 5-minute sessions.

### Step 5: Add 300 Core Words
Gradually learn the 300 most common words in context — not from flashcard lists, but within real sentences and conversations.

### Step 6: Start Speaking ImmediatelyZ
From Day 1, form sentences. Talk to yourself, talk to a partner, talk to an AI. Don't wait until you "know enough."

### Step 7: Don't Stop at 16 Hours
Use the Petrov method as your launchpad. After the foundation is set, continue with native content, conversation practice, reading, and expanded grammar study.
