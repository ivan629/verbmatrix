# THE POLYGLOT FRAMEWORK
## A Petrov-Faithful, Language-Agnostic System for Rapid Language Acquisition
### (Designed for use with AI lesson generation)

---

## 0. PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT

This is a complete, self-contained framework for acquiring **conversational competence in any language in ~16 hours of structured instruction**, based on Dmitry Petrov's "Polyglot 16" method, corrected and expanded with techniques he uses in practice but rarely lists explicitly.

**Two use cases:**
1. **As a learner's reference** — follow the principles, drill the matrix, build fluency.
2. **As an AI instruction set** — feed this entire document to an AI (Claude, GPT, Gemini) along with a target language, and request lessons built strictly to this framework. A ready-to-paste prompt template is in Section 11.

---

## 1. CORE PHILOSOPHY

### Petrov's foundational claim
> "Anyone who speaks their native language decently is, by definition, capable of speaking another one. Only a lack of motivation can be a limiter."

The barrier is **psychological, not intellectual**. Most language learning fails because of fear, perfectionism, and overwhelm — not lack of talent.

### The two non-negotiable rules
1. **Freedom before correctness.** Speak from minute one — badly, slowly, with errors. Correctness is polished later. Communication first.
2. **Regularity over volume.** 5 minutes × 5 times a day beats 2 hours once a week. The matrix wires itself into memory through frequent contact, not long sessions.

### The 90/10 rule
- ~300–350 words = 90% of everything anyone says in any language.
- ~50–60 verbs = 90% of all verb usage.
- ~10–15 grammar patterns = 90% of all sentence structures.

We learn ONLY the 90%. The remaining 10% takes years and comes later.

---

## 2. THE FOUR FOUNDATIONAL PILLARS (Petrov's Official Method)

These are the four pillars Petrov teaches at his center. **All four must be active simultaneously** — removing any one breaks the method.

### Pillar 1 — Comfortable, friendly atmosphere
The student must feel safe. No correction during early speaking. No shame. No fear of mistakes. The teacher (or AI) is a partner, not a judge.
**Petrov:** *"Language must not be feared. It must be loved."*

### Pillar 2 — Speak from Day One
Automate the basic algorithms (the verb matrix). Generate many combinations from few words. Apply the **frequency principle** — only highest-use words make it into the early lessons.

### Pillar 3 — Talk about YOURSELF (never abstract topics)
From Day 1, every drill is personalized. Not "the cat sits on the mat" — but "I work in Bucharest, I like coffee, my brother lives in Berlin." Personal content engages emotion; emotion locks in memory.

### Pillar 4 — Language as a multi-sensory living space
A language has a **voice, color, taste, and smell**. The learner builds a personal sensory association set (the "key bundle") for that language. Italian = espresso steam + Fellini + Vespa engine. This bundle becomes the password that loads the entire language structure when activated.

## 3. THE CENTRAL MATRIX — THE "MULTIPLICATION TABLE" OF LANGUAGE

Every sentence in any language fits into a 3×3 grid:

```
              | PAST       | PRESENT    | FUTURE
--------------|------------|------------|------------
AFFIRMATIVE + | I loved    | I love     | I will love
NEGATIVE   −  | I did not  | I do not   | I will not
              | love       | love       | love
QUESTION   ?  | Did I love?| Do I love? | Will I love?
```

**Universal observations (apply to ANY language):**
- One cell is "different" — the **affirmative past** — where the verb usually changes form (irregulars).
- Helper words (auxiliaries) appear in **negatives, questions, and future** — the base verb returns to its dictionary form.
- For 3rd person singular in present (he/she/it), there's often a small modification (English -s, Romance verb endings, etc.).

### Architectural rule
**The matrix stays at 9 cells. Always.**
The continuous/progressive form (-ing in English, gerund in Romance, etc.) is treated as a **separate parallel sub-matrix** introduced later. Do not combine them. This is what keeps the table manageable.

### How to build the matrix for ANY language
For each new language, answer these 9 questions:

| Cell | Question to answer |
|------|---------------------|
| Affirmative Present | What is the base form + person ending? |
| Negative Present | What word(s) make negation? Where do they go? |
| Question Present | How is a question formed — word order change, particle, intonation? |
| Affirmative Past | Suffix? Irregular? Auxiliary? |
| Negative Past | Same negator as present? Different? |
| Question Past | Different auxiliary? |
| Affirmative Future | Auxiliary verb? Suffix? Use of present + time word? |
| Negative Future | Position of negator? |
| Question Future | Word order? |

Once you have these 9 answers, you have the language's matrix.

## 4. THE DRILLING PROTOCOL (How to Reach Automatism)

### Mechanics
- Pick one verb.
- Run it through all 9 cells, **out loud**.
- Time: 20–30 seconds per verb.
- Move to the next verb. Repeat.

### Schedule
- 5-minute sessions, **3–5 times per day**.
- After 2–4 days, the structure starts firing automatically.
- After 7–10 days, you can build any sentence with these verbs without conscious thought.

### Why this works
The goal is NOT understanding — that takes minutes. The goal is **automatism**, the level at which 7×8=56 surfaces without computation. Reaching this requires repetition, not insight.

### Drill formats to rotate
1. **Pure conjugation** — verb through all 9 cells.
2. **Pronoun substitution** — same cell, all pronouns (I/you/he/she/we/they).
3. **Translation drill** — random Russian/English sentences → target language.
4. **Self-talk** — narrate your day using current verbs.
5. **Question chains** — generate 9 questions about a topic using the question column.

## 5. THE HIGH-FREQUENCY ASSET LISTS

These are the building blocks. **Master these before adding anything else.**

### The 30 Magic Verbs (starter set)
```

think, want, take, give, speak, tell, ask, make, do,
have, get, put, read, write, eat, drink, sleep, buy,

The full daily-use set is 50–60 verbs. The framework targets 30 in the first 8 lessons, expanding to 50–60 by Lesson 16.

People    | everybody  | somebody  | nobody
Things    | everything | something | nothing
Place     | everywhere | somewhere | nowhere
Time      | always     | sometimes | never
```

## 6. PEDAGOGICAL TECHNIQUES (The "Plumbing" Petrov Uses)

These are the techniques that turn drilled structures into actual fluency. **Most summaries of his method skip these. They are essential.**

### Technique 1 — Filler Words ("Parasites")
Native speech is 50–80% filler. Memorize 10 native fillers and use them shamelessly:

**English example:** *well, you know, I mean, sort of, like, actually, basically, pretty much, kind of, honestly*

Fillers do TWO jobs:
1. Buy your brain time to assemble the next clause.
2. Make you sound fluent even when you're not.

Petrov's demonstration: a 13-word "fluent" English answer can be 12 fillers + 1 content word — and still pass as natural.

### Technique 2 — The Actor / Persona
Pick a character or object that **embodies** the language. When you speak, you "become" them. This breaks the "Russian-person-trying-to-speak-English" self-consciousness loop.

Examples:
- For Italian: become a Roman waiter at a trattoria.
- For French: become a Parisian intellectual at a café.
- For English: become a confident American startup founder.

### Technique 3 — The Sensory Key Bundle
For each language, build a personal bundle of 5–10 sensory anchors:
- A song
- A food smell or taste
- A film or scene
- A city or street image
- A famous voice
- A typical sound (Vespa, double-decker, rain on tatami)

When you want to "switch into" the language, mentally pick up the bundle. This is also how you keep multiple languages from bleeding into each other.

### Technique 4 — Reformulate, Never Stop
When a word doesn't come, **do not apologize** and break flow. Reformulate:

- ❌ "Sorry, I can't remember the word…"
- ✅ "I forgot the word for that thing — you know, the one we saw on TV, in the football game, last week, it was big…"

Maintain comfort. The matrix keeps producing grammar; vocabulary gaps are filled by description.

### Technique 5 — Films You Already Know, No Subtitles
Specific recommendation, not just "watch native content":
- The film must be one whose plot you **already know well** (re-watching).
- Subtitles **OFF** — they steal attention from audio.
- Children's cartoons in target language work well for early learners.

### Technique 6 — Avoid News Broadcasts as Listening Material
News is scripted, edited, and unnatural. Use instead:
- Talk shows
- Interviews
- Podcasts
- Vlogs
- Stand-up comedy

These contain real conversational rhythm and filler words.

### Technique 7 — The -tion/-sion Cognate Hack
~50,000 abstract nouns in major languages share Latin/Greek roots and predictable endings. Learn the transformation rule and instantly gain thousands of words:

| English | French | Spanish | Italian | Portuguese | Russian |
|---------|--------|---------|---------|------------|---------|
| -tion   | -tion  | -ción   | -zione  | -ção       | -ция    |
| -sion   | -sion  | -sión   | -sione  | -são       | -сия    |

*information / révolution / situation* → all near-identical across these languages.

### Technique 8 — Conditional Clauses Always Use Present
Universal rule for many Indo-European languages: in *"When/if I X, I will Y"*, the X is in PRESENT tense, even though it refers to future. Don't load up future tense in both clauses.

### Technique 9 — Phrasal Verbs / Verb + Particle
In English especially, **phrasal verbs are essential, not optional**: *go up, look back, take out, get away, put on*. Native speech is saturated with them. Drill them as separate units.

### Technique 10 — Greetings & Etiquette Block
Treat as one module: hello, goodbye, please, thank you, sorry, excuse me, nice to meet you, you're welcome, see you later. Memorize the whole block in the first 2 hours.

## 7. THE CORRECTED 16-LESSON ROADMAP

Here is what each lesson should actually contain, corrected against actual Polyglot course content:

### PHASE A — Foundation (Lessons 1–4)

**Lesson 1 — The Verb Matrix**
- Psychological warm-up: identify motivation, build sensory key bundle.
- Introduce 3×3 matrix (past/present/future × +/−/?).
- First 9 verbs: love, live, work, open, close, see, come, go, know.
- 3 irregulars introduced (see/saw, come/came, go/went, know/knew).
- Homework: drill the table to automatism, 5 min × multiple sessions.

**Lesson 2 — Pronouns, Question Words, Prepositions**
- Pronouns in 2 forms (subject + object).
- 6 question words (what/who/where/when/why/how).
- Basic prepositions (in, to, from).
- 8 new verbs: ask, answer, give, take, help, hope, speak, travel.
- Introduce filler words and the -tion/-sion cognate hack.
- Demonstrate that 15–20 words generate ~5,000 sentence combinations.

**Lesson 3 — The Verb "To Be"**
- Separate matrix for the copula (am/is/are, was/were, will be).
- Introduce the continuous/progressive sub-matrix (to be + -ing) as a parallel structure.
- 3rd group of pronouns: possessives (my/your/his/her/our/their).
- 7 high-frequency adjectives: hungry, pretty, fat, crazy, cute, skinny, in love.
- Greetings & etiquette block.

**Lesson 4 — Talking About Yourself**
- Personal introductions: name, profession, location, family.
- Articles (a/an, the).
- More prepositions (with, without, about, for).
- Profession & workplace vocabulary.
- First sustained personal monologue (1–2 minutes about yourself).

### PHASE B — Building Blocks (Lessons 5–8)

**Lesson 5 — Comparative Degrees + Time Vocabulary**
- old/older/oldest, big/bigger/biggest, good/better/best, bad/worse/worst.
- More/most for long adjectives.
- Days, months, seasons, time prepositions.
- Time markers: yesterday/today/tomorrow, ago, in X days, this/next/last.

**Lesson 6 — Dialogues, much/many, the Universal Coverage Table**
- Active dialogue practice (Q→A→Q chains).
- much vs many, little vs few.
- The everyone/something/nowhere/never grid.
- Double-negation rule: in English, only ONE negative per sentence.

**Lesson 7 — Simplified Matrix + 10 New Verbs**
- Compressed/shortcut version of the verb matrix.
- 5 irregular verbs: buy/bought, sell/sold, pay/paid, make/made, choose/chose.
- 5 regular verbs: try, change, show, play, turn.
- Imperative mood: go!, don't go!, let's go!

**Lesson 8 — Prepositions of Place/Direction + Phrasal Verbs**
- Spatial prepositions with visual diagrams.
- Postpositions: up, down, back, away, in, out, on, off, over, through.
- Phrasal verb construction: go up, look back, take out, get away, etc.
- ~10 phrasal verbs × 18 particles ≈ 180 new "compound verbs."

### PHASE C — Real Conversation (Lessons 9–12)

**Lesson 9 — Storytelling & Reflexive Pronouns**
- Real-life story narration in target language.
- Reflexive pronouns: myself, yourself, himself, herself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves.
- Indirect questions (Do you know what time it is?).
- Adverbs of frequency: always, sometimes, usually, often, seldom, never.

**Lesson 10 — Cultural Topics & Discussion**
- Free discussion on art, films, books, food, travel.
- Synonyms and avoiding tautology.
- "On the one hand… on the other hand…" structure.
- Word: both, neither, all, none.

**Lesson 11 — Verbal Aspect & Translation Challenges**
- Russian perfective/imperfective vs English simple/continuous/perfect.
- How to express completed vs ongoing actions.
- Workarounds when direct translation fails.
- Synonym pairs: knew/found out, was able to/succeeded, etc.

**Lesson 12 — Modal Verbs**
- can/could (ability), must/should (obligation), may/might (possibility).
- Polite requests: would you, could you, may I.
- "Have to," "ought to," "need to."

### PHASE D — Consolidation (Lessons 13–16)

**Lesson 13 — Complex Sentences & Conditionals**
- Subordinate clauses: that, which, who, because, although, if.
- 1st conditional: if + present, will + verb.
- Light introduction to 2nd conditional (if + past, would + verb).

**Lesson 14 — Present Perfect / Past Perfect (Light Intro)**
- have/has + past participle.
- Difference: "I lived" vs "I have lived" vs "I had lived."
- For/since/already/yet/just.

**Lesson 15 — Passive Voice + Reported Speech**
- "The book was written by…" structure.
- "He said that he was tired" → reported speech.
- Sequence of tenses.

**Lesson 16 — Integration & Free Expression**
- Extended conversation on any topic (15+ minutes).
- Build personal continuation plan: which films, podcasts, books, conversation partners.
- Confirm: matrix is automatic, 50–60 verbs internalized, 300+ words active.

## 8. LANGUAGE-AGNOSTIC ADAPTATION RULES

For any new target language, follow these steps to build your custom version of the framework.

### Step A — Identify the matrix
Answer the 9 cell-questions in Section 3. Build the language's specific 9-cell table.

### Step B — Identify gotchas
| Language type | Special handling |
|---|---|
| Tonal (Chinese, Vietnamese, Thai) | Add tone marking to ALL vocabulary. Drill tones from Day 1, never separately later. |
| Case-heavy (Russian, German, Latin, Polish, Finnish) | Pronouns must be drilled in ALL their case forms. The pronoun table expands from 3 to 4–7 columns. |
| Script-different (Arabic, Hebrew, Hindi, Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Thai, Georgian) | Use transliteration for the first 4 lessons. Introduce script gradually from Lesson 5. Do NOT delay speaking until script is mastered. |
| Verb-aspect (Slavic) | The matrix expands: each verb has perfective + imperfective pair. Drill both from the start. |
| Verb-agglutinative (Turkish, Finnish, Hungarian, Korean) | The matrix is mostly suffix concatenation. Drill suffix slots, not whole forms. |
| Articles (Romance, Germanic) | Add article rules to Lesson 4. |
| No articles (Slavic, East Asian) | Skip article instruction entirely. |
| Honorifics (Japanese, Korean) | Pick ONE register for Lessons 1–8 (polite-formal default). Add registers later. |

### Step C — Find the high-frequency lists
Use a frequency dictionary (most languages have one published). Take:
- Top 30 verbs
- Top 100 nouns
- Top 50 adjectives
- Top 6 question words
- Top 20 prepositions

### Step D — Build the cognate map
Identify what your learner's L1 shares with the target language:
- Latin/Greek roots (most European languages)
- Sanskrit roots (Indian languages)
- Arabic roots (Persian, Turkish, Swahili, Malay)
- English loanwords (Japanese カタカナ, Korean, Chinese tech vocab)

This produces an instant "free vocabulary" bonus of 1,000–10,000 words.

### Step E — Build the sensory key bundle
Choose 5–10 cultural anchors specific to the target language.

## 9. THE 30 IMMUTABLE RULES (DO / DON'T)

### DO
1. Speak from minute one — even broken speech counts.
2. Drill the matrix 5 minutes × multiple times per day.
3. Personalize EVERY sentence (talk about yourself).
4. Use fillers shamelessly.
5. Treat -ing/continuous as a separate sub-matrix.
6. Learn pronoun sets together (subject + object + possessive).
7. Build a sensory key bundle BEFORE starting lessons.
8. Watch known films without subtitles.
9. Reformulate when stuck — never apologize and stop.
10. Add irregulars as exceptions, never delay starting because of them.
11. Use cognates aggressively.
12. Memorize question words and prepositions as blocks.
13. Adopt an actor persona.
14. Treat etiquette as one memorized chunk.
15. Combine verbs with pronouns immediately (don't drill them separately).

### DON'T
1. Don't teach phonetics/IPA before speaking.
2. Don't correct every mistake — kills the comfort pillar.
3. Don't use news broadcasts as listening material.
4. Don't watch with subtitles in the early phase.
5. Don't introduce more than 8–10 new verbs per lesson.
6. Don't expand the matrix beyond 9 cells.
7. Don't teach abstract topics in Lessons 1–4 (must be personal).
8. Don't translate word-for-word — translate meaning.
9. Don't worry about accent in the first 16 hours.
10. Don't drill silently — always speak out loud.
11. Don't postpone speaking until "you know enough."
12. Don't focus on a regional accent unless you live in that region.
13. Don't use flashcards alone — use words in sentences.
14. Don't load up two future-tense verbs in conditional clauses.
15. Don't separate "personal" from "object" from "possessive" pronoun chapters.

## 10. LESSON QUALITY CHECKLIST

A well-built lesson, AI-generated or human-built, must contain ALL of these:

- [ ] Clear lesson topic and prerequisites (which prior lessons assumed).
- [ ] 5–10 new high-frequency words (max).
- [ ] 1 grammar structure (max), tied to the matrix.
- [ ] At least 10 personalized drill sentences (about the learner).
- [ ] At least 5 dialogue Q→A pairs.
- [ ] One sensory anchor reference (link to the key bundle).
- [ ] Fillers used in example sentences (model native rhythm).
- [ ] One cognate hack if available.
- [ ] One real-world micro-task: "by tomorrow, say X in conversation."
- [ ] Clear connection back to the verb matrix.

## 13. WHAT THIS FRAMEWORK DELIVERS AND WHAT IT DOESN'T

### Delivers
- Conversational survival in 16 hours.
- A "fireproof" foundation that survives years of disuse.
- The cognitive infrastructure to expand into any depth later.
- Confidence — the elimination of language fear.

### Doesn't deliver (be honest with yourself)
- Fluency. That takes 1–3 years of additional practice.
- Reading literature. Requires 3,000+ words.
- Professional or academic register. Requires specialized study.
- Native-like pronunciation. Requires immersion or a coach.

This is a **launchpad**, not a finish line. Petrov's own statement:
> "To master a language perfectly, a lifetime is not enough.
> But to learn to understand people and be understood — that takes only a few days."
